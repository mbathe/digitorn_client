It has already been said that there have been but three major type families in the history of Western printing: (1) [black letter](https://www.britannica.com/topic/black-letter), commonly and not quite rightly called Gothic by the English; (2) [roman](https://www.britannica.com/topic/roman-typeface), in [Germany](https://www.britannica.com/place/Germany) still called by its historical name of Antiqua; and (3) [italic](https://www.britannica.com/topic/italic). All had their origin in the scripts of the [calligraphers](https://www.britannica.com/art/calligraphy) whose work [printing](https://www.britannica.com/topic/printing-publishing) came ultimately to replace.\n\n[Calligraphy](https://www.britannica.com/art/calligraphy) is dealt with at length in other articles (_see also_ [calligraphy](https://www.britannica.com/topic/writing)). It is necessary here only to provide a [context](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/context) for the evolution of the typefaces of the printer’s [font](https://www.britannica.com/technology/font). The basic letter forms of the [Latin alphabet](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Latin-alphabet) were established by the classical imperial [capital letters](https://www.britannica.com/topic/majuscule) of 1st-century [Rome](https://www.britannica.com/place/Rome). [Lowercase letters](https://www.britannica.com/topic/minuscule) emerged only slowly, with their most vigorous development coming between the 6th and 8th centuries.\n\n[Charlemagne](https://www.britannica.com/art/Carolingian-art), in order to encourage standardization and discourage further experimentation, ordered his educational program for the [Holy Roman Empire](https://www.britannica.com/place/Holy-Roman-Empire) to be written in a script consisting of roman capitals and a specific form of minuscules (lowercase letters) known as [Carolingian minuscule](https://www.britannica.com/art/Carolingian-minuscule). The uniformity thus achieved was short-lived. Under the impact of the national and regional styles of the [scribes](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/scribes) who worked with the alphabet, the letters—clear, simple, and somewhat broad by today’s standards—were gradually compressed laterally, until, by the 11th century, the curves had been converted to points and angles, and the body of the letter had been made thinner while the strokes of which it was composed had been made thicker. This was [black letter](https://www.britannica.com/topic/black-letter). By the 15th century it had completed its evolution into the formal, square-text Gothic letter.\n\nIt was this formal black letter that provided the first model for printer’s type when printing was invented.
